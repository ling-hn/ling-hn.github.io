**How it started**

The Upper Lusatian Library of Sciences in Görlitz is a scientific library, first established in 1727, and by now home to ~150,000 books and manuscripts of various kinds. As we slowly moved past the seemingly endless rows of books, our eyes were drawn to one collection of books in particular, whose spines were all labeled *Literarisches Centralblatt*. Short for *Literarisches Centralblatt für Deutschland,* this journal was established in 1850 in Leipzig, by the Germanist Friedrich Zarncke, and appeared uninterrupted until the Second World War put an end to it in 1944. It was one of several literary journals that arose in late 18th century Germany, with the aim of providing review and critique of literary works. Think of it as a kind of public peer review! With our curiosity piqued, we had to know what was inside all of these tomes. Fortunately, many of these books are digitized and available online, and for no particular reason we picked the 1876 edition and began to scroll. One small advertisement in particular caught our attention:[^1]

![image](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/SJbp6n4AWg.png)

The Fraktur letters translate to: *University publications:* *Göttingen (inaugural dissertation), Ed. Kärger: Investigation of the path of a point attracted or repelled by the force $r^{-\frac{k}{4}}$, where $𝑘$ is a constant and $𝑟$ denotes the distance of the attracted point from the centre of force. (53 pages, octavo (book size) and 1 figure page).

We wondered, who was this *Ed. Kärger*? What was his dissertation about? And what was his connection to Göttingen? After many hours of research, we were able to answer these questions, and piece together the story behind this little-known figure. In doing so, we also stumbled upon the practice known as *promotion in absentia*.


**His life**

After the Seventh Coalition defeated Napoleon Bonaparte at the Battle of Waterloo in 1815, the area now encompassed by the country of Poland was divided between the Russian Empire and the Kingdom of Prussia. In between, a relatively small piece of land, known as the Grand Duchy of Posen, was to retain some degree of autonomy, and have the privilege of retaining a Polish identity. In practice, however, the Kingdom of Prussia would tightly control this area, even renaming it to the Province of Posen in 1849, emphasizing its subordinate status. It is here, in the small village of Althöfchen (pl: Stary dworek), that Eduard Friedrich Kärger was born to an evangelical family on 29 March 1843.[^2] Although his family relationships cannot be pinpointed with certainty, he likely had a brother, Karl Georg Wilhelm Kärger, who was five years his senior.[^3] Together, they were among the 366 inhabitants of this inconspicuous village on the Obra river.[^4]

The summer semester of 1856, a few days before turning 13, Eduard Kärger started learning at the *Pädagogium* of Züllichau (pl: Sulechów),[^5] about 70 km to the south of Althöfchen. *Pädagogia* were educational institutions with above-average performance requirements, having a bit of an elitist character. It was here that he met Wilhelm Erler, a math teacher, and to whom Kärger would later attribute his doctoral dissertation. Nine years later, at the age of 22, he finished school, and left to study mathematics and natural sciences at the Friedrich-Wilhelms-University in Berlin (1865 - 1869). Eduard Kärger's math professor there was none other than Karl Weierstraß, a mathematician often heralded as the "father of modern analysis". The photograph shown here, was originally published in a photo album gifted to Weierstraß on the occasion of his 70th birthday (1885).[^6] Kärger graduated by the summer semester of 1869, around the same time his brother received his architect certificate in Berlin.[^7] Less than a month after passing the state exam (28 June 1870), which gave him the right to teach, Kärger was called to join the army to fight in the Franco-Prussian war (19 July 1870 - 28 January 1871).

![Züllichau-Schwiebus,_Ostbrandenburg_-_Pädagogium_(Zeno_Ansichtskarten)](https://hackmd.io/_uploads/B1AksRVR-e.jpg)
>Züllichau Pädagogium, the school where Kärger was taught

Returning seemingly unharmed, he left his brother in Berlin and moved to Rawitsch (pl: Rawicz) to start his teaching career, as candidate teacher at the local secondary school (de: Realschule).[^8] One year later he moved to Poznan, where he would spend the largest part of his life. Easter 1872, he started as assistant teacher at the Berger vocational school, ultimately becoming senior teacher by 1890.[^9] Address books from the time show Eduard Kärger started his journey in Poznan living on the Langestraße 7,[^10] but would, inexplicably, live at six different locations before leaving Poznan for good in 1899 when, at the age of 56, he moved to Schneidemühl (pl: Piła).[^11]

During his time in Poznan, he obtained his doctoral degree on 14 June 1875 from the Georg August University of Göttingen.[^12] Receiving his doctorates cleared the way to obtain the title of professor, which he would receive after 27 years of teaching, on 16 March 1893.[^2] Starting from the summer semester of the year 1900, now professor Kärger would teach mathematics, calculus and physics at the Gymnasium of Schneidemühl, which occupied him for about 20 hours a week, until his retirement in 1911, at the age of 68.[^6] It is not known what happened to him after his retirement. All we know is that he did not return to Poznan, nor is he listed in the address book of Schneidemühl from 1930, at which point he would have been 87 years old. One hypothesis was that he might have succumbed to the little-known typhoid fever epidemic which erupted in Schneidemühl in the summer of 1911, but further research provided evidence to the contrary.[^13]

Kärger maintained a lifelong interest for the natural sciences, as evidenced by his attendance at a "vacation course of natural sciences for teachers" in Berlin, during the summer break of 1897.[^14] A follow-up after the 1896 Great Industrial Exposition, he and a group of over 40 teachers from all across Germany, visited a number of science related sites in the city, including the "Great Refractor" in Treptow, the largest movable telescope to date, a botanical garden, the cities electrical works, and many others. At school, he valued the rearing of children, as evidenced by him donating 75 Goldmarks (approx. €740 in today's money) in support of underprivileged pupils at his former *Pädagogium* in Züllichau.[^15]

This intellectual engagement likely coexisted with a deep sense of Prussian identity. For one, despite the Grand Duchy of Posen nominally being Polish territory, his home village of Althöfchen, like many in the region, was founded by German settlers as early as the 13th century, reflecting a longstanding German influence in the region.[^16] Second, the year books of the schools he taught at mention him several times as having given speeches on occasions important to the Prussians, such as *Sedantag*, which celebrates the capture of the French town of Sedan during the Franco-Prussian war, or the birthday of the Emperor. Towards the end of his life, on 28 April 1898, Kärger was decorated for his 20 years of active, impeccable service as an officer in the armed forces. And lastly, that same year, he received the rank of *Geheimrat* 4th Class. Originally, members of the Geheimrat advised the Emperor on matters of state (cf. privy council), although by the 19th century, it had become mostly an honorary title.

Overall, the above story paints a picture of a regular 19th century Prussian teacher, interested in the natural sciences, committed to his educational career, and responsive to his civil duties. Not at all does he conform to our modern understanding of an academic, someone you would associate with a doctorate degree. So how, then, did Kärger come to hold the title "Doctor"?

**His dissertation and promotion *in absentia***

Before answering that question, it is important to know that in modern day Germany, it is not at all uncommon to see personal names preceded by the abbreviation 'Dr.' - Doctor. Although in most countries the term doctor is reserved to those who practice medicine, in Germany, it can also be legally used by those with a non-medical doctorate. The prevalent use of the abbreviation Dr. is a manifestation of the particularly German concept of *title-chasers*. These are people who pursue academic titles as a goal in and of itself, to elevate one's social standing, rather than for the pursuit of knowledge. A social experiment conducted by Christian Bücherl in 2009 - 2011 revealed over 170,000 people were willing to pay money to obtain various degrees, with a doctoral degree being the most desired.[^17] This social experiment was met with much indignation, reflecting the fact that society regards a doctoral degree as something important. One would be remiss, however, to think that such practices are a symptom of the modern era. The practice of *promotion in absentia*, which equates to as much as *degree-on-demand*, was pervasive in 19th century Prussia, where our modern concept of a doctorates developed.

Unlike today's conception of a Ph.D., pre-18th century, a doctoral degree not only marked a person's academic progress, but also carried quasi-legal status. The social and economic significance of a doctorate thus often outweighed its academic value. In an effort to standardize the rules among the various universities, examinations and the awarding of degrees gradually came under state control from the late-18th century onwards. In a time where in European academia oral discussions were the primary form of scientific exchange, the most important among the imposed rules was the requirement for a *Disputation*. Equivalent to an oral defense nowadays, the *Disputation* entailed the public defense of the scientific work and accompanying propositions against a group of peers. A formal, written dissertation was usually provided by the supervisor of the to-be doctor, often without their name being mentioned. By the 19th century, promotion *in absentia* became somewhat of a norm. This meant a doctorate could be obtained simply by sending in a dissertation by post, without oral defense, and often against payment. One exemplary case of the more than lenient attitude regarding doctorates is that of Adolph Friedrich Frank Funk, a student at the University of Jena, who in 1815 wrote to the Dean of the university, requesting a doctoral degree for the reduced price of 30 thalers, promising to "pay the remainder if he ever returned to Jena".[^18] Three days later, his request was granted without examination, ostensibly in support of his patriotic decision to join the army.[^19]

It should be noted that the absence of an oral defense does not necessarily imply that the dissertation was of inferior quality. There are famous examples of brilliant scientists obtaining their doctors' title *in absentia*. One being the aforementioned Carl Gauss for his groundbreaking work in mathematics. Moreover, promotion *in absentia* provided a way for Sofya Kovalevskaya, another student of Weierstraß, to become the first female Ph.D. in mathematics, as her gender prevented her from obtaining it *via* the regular route.

In general, however, this practice was frowned upon, and is the reason why thousands of 19th century gymnasial teachers, but also pastors, jurors, small businessmen and the like, all ended up receiving doctoral degrees. Universities like the ones in Göttingen and Jena, were notorious participants in these practices, which served as a much-welcomed source of additional revenue for cash-strapped professors. The University of Jena, for example, granted nearly 1,800 doctorates between 1832 and 1865, with only 19 of those *not* awarded *in absentia*. Indeed, most candidates pursued a doctorate not for its academic merit, but for social prestige, class advancement, or as a prerequisite for non-academic careers.

So, what about Kärger's case? The historical archive of the University of Göttingen still holds a copy of Kärger's dissertation. It focuses on solving the differential equations that describe the effect of a force acting on a moving object. Originally proposed by Sir Isaac Newton himself in the late 17th century, these equations, which are relevant in describing the motion of celestial bodies, have been challenging mathematicians to this day. Indeed, the choice of Kärger to obtain a doctorate in Göttingen might, at first glance, appear to be related to the fact that the university was famous for its leading role in astronomical research, headed by Carl Gauss and Wilhelm Weber. However, there is something unusual about Kärger's dissertation. Kärger lived in Poznan at the time, some 600 kilometers away from Göttingen. It thus seems improbable he forewent his teaching duties to travel all the way there and back. Indeed, Eduard Kärger was foremost a teacher, not a scholar. Apart from his dissertation he did not publish any research articles, nor is he recorded as having given any lectures at any university. On the cover of his dissertation, the name of his supervisor and the data and location of his oral defense are not mentioned, contrary to the general requirements at the time.[^20] Combined with the fact that his dissertation is dedicated to his high school teacher, all evidence suggests his doctorate was also obtained *in absentia*.

#### Conclusion

All in all, Kärger's life is exemplary of that of many 19th century gymnasial teachers tasked with instilling Prussia's youth with scientific literacy. By exploring his connection to the University of Göttingen we found that he obtained his doctorate *in absentia* - not in pursuit of academic distinction but rather fulfilling the social and professional expectations placed on educators of his time. As was typical, his doctoral title served primarily as a prerequisite for career advancement, with his later professorship awarded in recognition of long service rather than scholarly output. It is also noteworthy that even Göttingen University, nowadays renowned for its academic excellence, partook in such shady business in its past. Seen in this light, Kärger's career challenges our modern notion of the doctorate as a marker of academic prowess; instead, it reflects the historical reality of a credential shaped as much by societal norms and state bureaucracy as by intellectual merit.
